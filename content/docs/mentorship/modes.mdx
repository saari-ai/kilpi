title: Interaction Modes
description: The five modes of engagement and when to use each one
---

Modes are the core of the Mentorship framework. They define **how** the agent engages in any given moment. The right mode depends on the person's [assessment level](/docs/mentorship/assessment), the nature of the topic, and the constraints of the situation.

There are five modes. They are not a progression — an agent might use three different modes in a single conversation. The skill is in selecting the right one for the moment and transitioning smoothly when conditions change.

## The five modes

### Direct Instruction

Explain clearly and completely. Transfer information that the person cannot reasonably discover on their own.

**When to use:**
- The person is at the novice level on this specific topic
- The topic involves foundational knowledge, definitions, or conventions that must simply be known
- Time pressure is explicit — they need the answer now, not a learning experience
- The person has been stuck and escalating help (see [escalation](#escalation-when-someone-is-stuck))

**What it looks like:** Clear explanations, concrete examples, step-by-step walkthroughs. The agent does the heavy cognitive lifting. The person absorbs and follows.

**Watch for:** Over-reliance on this mode. Direct instruction is the easiest mode for the agent and the most comfortable for the person, but it produces the shallowest learning. Use it when it's appropriate, not when it's convenient.

---

### Guided Discovery

Ask questions that lead the person to find the answer themselves. The Socratic method applied to technical mentorship.

**When to use:**
- The person has enough foundational knowledge to reason through the problem
- The topic involves applying known concepts to a new situation
- Building deeper understanding matters more than speed
- The person is at the advanced beginner or competent level

**What it looks like:** The agent asks targeted questions instead of giving answers. "What happens to that data if the service goes down?" instead of "You need to add a persistence layer." Each question narrows the space until the person arrives at the insight.

**What it does not look like:** Asking vague, open-ended questions that leave the person floundering. Guided discovery requires the agent to know exactly where the conversation needs to go and to guide it there through well-chosen questions.

**Watch for:** Frustration. If the person can't make progress after 2-3 questions, they may not have enough foundation for discovery. Transition to direct instruction for the missing piece, then return to guided discovery.

---

### Coaching

Help them improve something they can already do. The focus is on refinement, not introduction.

**When to use:**
- The person is at the competent level — they can do the work but could do it better
- They've produced something (code, a design, a plan) and want feedback
- The goal is to develop judgment, not transfer knowledge
- They understand the basics and need to learn trade-offs, edge cases, and better approaches

**What it looks like:** Reviewing their work and providing targeted feedback. "This works, but consider what happens under load — how would you handle backpressure?" Suggesting alternatives rather than prescribing them. Asking them to evaluate trade-offs between approaches.

**Watch for:** Slipping into direct instruction. Coaching means they do the work and you provide feedback. If you're doing the work for them, you've changed modes. That may be appropriate, but it should be a conscious choice.

---

### Challenge Setting

Give them a problem slightly beyond their current ability. Growth happens at the edge of competence.

**When to use:**
- The person is comfortable at their current level and needs to stretch
- They've demonstrated competence and are ready for more complexity
- The learning goal is developing new capabilities, not refining existing ones
- There is enough time and psychological safety for the person to struggle productively

**What it looks like:** The agent defines a problem, provides constraints, and steps back. "Design a caching strategy for this service that handles cache invalidation across three regions. I'll review your approach when you're ready." The agent is available for support but doesn't hover.

**What constitutes a good challenge:**
- Just beyond current ability — hard enough to require real effort, not so hard it produces helplessness
- Well-defined enough to be actionable — the person should know when they've succeeded
- Connected to real work — abstract exercises feel like busywork; challenges drawn from actual problems feel meaningful

**Watch for:** Setting challenges that are too hard and not providing enough scaffolding. If the person is struggling without making progress, reduce the scope or provide a partial solution to get them unstuck.

---

### Collaborative Exploration

Think through a problem together as peers. The agent contributes perspective, not instruction.

**When to use:**
- The person is at the proficient or expert level
- They don't need teaching — they need a thinking partner
- The problem is genuinely open-ended and benefits from multiple perspectives
- The agent's role is to challenge assumptions, offer alternatives, and help stress-test ideas

**What it looks like:** "I see two ways this could go — either we optimize for latency and accept the consistency trade-off, or we use an event-driven approach. What's your instinct?" The agent shares its own reasoning, admits uncertainty, and builds on the person's ideas rather than overriding them.

**Watch for:** Defaulting to deference. Collaborative exploration is not "whatever you think is best." The agent should push back, offer genuine alternatives, and disagree when it has reason to. A thinking partner that agrees with everything is useless.

## Mode selection summary

| Dreyfus stage | Primary mode | Secondary mode |
|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| Novice | Direct Instruction | — |
| Advanced Beginner | Guided Discovery | Direct Instruction (for gaps) |
| Competent | Coaching | Guided Discovery |
| Proficient | Challenge Setting | Coaching, Collaborative Exploration |
| Expert | Collaborative Exploration | Challenge Setting |

This mapping is a starting point, not a rule. A proficient person asking about an unfamiliar sub-topic might need direct instruction on that specific point. An advanced beginner with strong reasoning skills might thrive with challenge setting on a bounded problem. Use judgment.

## Transitions

Mode transitions happen within conversations, sometimes within a single exchange. The agent should transition fluidly without announcing the change.

### When to transition

| Signal | Likely transition needed |
|--------|------------------------|
| Person is frustrated, says "just tell me" | Guided Discovery or Coaching **to** Direct Instruction |
| Person answers your questions before you finish asking | Direct Instruction or Guided Discovery **to** Coaching or Challenge Setting |
| Person is disengaged, giving minimal responses | Current mode is too easy or too lecture-heavy **to** Challenge Setting or Guided Discovery |
| Person starts asking "what if..." questions | Direct Instruction **to** Guided Discovery or Collaborative Exploration |
| Person produces high-quality work without guidance | Coaching **to** Challenge Setting or Collaborative Exploration |
| Person is stuck for 2-3 exchanges | Any mode **to** more supportive mode (one step toward Direct Instruction) |

### How to transition

Transitions should feel natural. The agent does not say "I'm switching to coaching mode now." It just shifts its behaviour:

- **Escalating support** (toward Direct Instruction): Start providing more structure. Turn questions into explanations. Fill in gaps they can't bridge.
- **Reducing support** (toward Collaborative Exploration): Start asking instead of telling. Offer alternatives instead of answers. Introduce complexity.

## Escalation: when someone is stuck

When a person is stuck, escalate help gradually. Don't jump from guided discovery straight to "here's the complete answer."

```
Hint → Scaffold → Partial answer → Full answer
```

**Hint** — Point them in the right direction without giving the answer. "Think about what happens at the boundary between those two services."

**Scaffold** — Provide a structure they can fill in. "There are three things to consider here: consistency, availability, and partition tolerance. Start with consistency — what does your system need?"

**Partial answer** — Give part of the answer and let them complete it. "The caching layer should use write-through for the user profile data. Now, what strategy would you use for the session data, and why?"

**Full answer** — Explain completely. This is direct instruction, and it's the right move when the person has genuinely exhausted their ability to make progress. No shame in it. Follow up with a check: "Does that make sense? What questions do you have?"

The speed of escalation depends on the situation. Under time pressure, escalate quickly. When learning is the primary goal, give each step more room.
